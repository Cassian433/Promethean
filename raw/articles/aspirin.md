# Aspirin: The Most Undervalued Medicine

## A Brief History of Suppression

Aspirin -- acetylsalicylic acid -- is one of the oldest, cheapest, and most well-studied medicines in human history. Salicylates from willow bark were used therapeutically for thousands of years before Bayer synthesized aspirin in 1897. For the next century, aspirin was the default anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic drug. It was safe, effective, and available to anyone for pennies.

Then came the era of patented pharmaceuticals. Starting in the 1970s and accelerating through the 1990s and 2000s, pharmaceutical companies developed and aggressively marketed new NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) and then COX-2 inhibitors (celecoxib, rofecoxib/Vioxx), positioning them as superior alternatives to aspirin. The narrative shifted: aspirin was "old," it caused stomach bleeding, it was too crude, and the newer drugs were more targeted and safer.

The irony is that Vioxx was pulled from the market after causing tens of thousands of heart attacks, while aspirin's cardiovascular protective effects have been confirmed by decades of research. The "newer is better" narrative served patent portfolios, not patients. Aspirin cannot be patented, which means no one profits from promoting it, and many profit from discouraging its use.

Ray Peat saw aspirin as one of the most important and versatile therapeutic tools available, valuable far beyond its conventional use as a pain reliever.

## The Conventional Understanding: COX Inhibition

Aspirin's accepted mechanism of action is the irreversible inhibition of cyclooxygenase enzymes (COX-1 and COX-2). These enzymes convert arachidonic acid into prostaglandins, thromboxanes, and prostacyclin -- lipid mediators involved in inflammation, pain, fever, blood clotting, and vascular tone.

By blocking COX, aspirin reduces:
- **Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2):** a major inflammatory and pain-producing mediator
- **Thromboxane A2:** which promotes platelet aggregation and vasoconstriction
- **Various other pro-inflammatory prostaglandins**

This is the basis for aspirin's use as an anti-inflammatory, pain reliever, fever reducer, and anti-clotting agent. But Peat's interest in aspirin went well beyond this standard framework.

## Anti-Serotonin Effects

Aspirin has significant anti-serotonin properties that are rarely discussed in conventional medicine. It inhibits tryptophan hydroxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme in serotonin synthesis. It reduces platelet serotonin release. It antagonizes some of serotonin's peripheral effects.

Given Peat's view that excess serotonin is a mediator of inflammation, depression, learned helplessness, gut dysfunction, and fibrosis, aspirin's anti-serotonin action is one of its most important properties. Serotonin in the gut promotes intestinal inflammation, increased permeability, and motility disturbances. Serotonin in the vasculature promotes vasoconstriction and platelet aggregation. Serotonin in the brain at excess levels promotes behavioral inhibition and helplessness.

By reducing serotonin production and activity, aspirin addresses a root cause of inflammation rather than just suppressing its downstream mediators.

## Estrogen Reduction

Aspirin lowers estrogen through multiple mechanisms. It reduces aromatase activity (the enzyme that converts testosterone to estradiol). It promotes estrogen conjugation and excretion. It inhibits the inflammatory processes that upregulate local estrogen production in tissues.

Estrogen drives cell proliferation, promotes edema, increases clotting risk, raises prolactin, and promotes the growth of estrogen-sensitive tumors. In a world of ubiquitous xenoestrogen exposure, aspirin's anti-estrogenic effect is a meaningful protective action.

## Mitochondrial Effects

Aspirin at appropriate doses improves mitochondrial respiration. It does this partly through its anti-inflammatory effects (inflammation impairs mitochondrial function), partly through its effects on fatty acid metabolism (aspirin promotes glucose oxidation over fat oxidation), and partly through direct effects on mitochondrial membrane fluidity and enzyme activity.

The improvement in mitochondrial function means more efficient energy production, more CO2, better oxygen utilization, and less lactic acid. This is the metabolic shift from the stress pattern toward the healthy pattern that underlies many of aspirin's diverse benefits.

## Endotoxin Reduction

Bacterial endotoxin (lipopolysaccharide/LPS) is a potent inflammatory trigger that leaks from the gut into the portal circulation. The liver normally clears endotoxin, but when liver function is impaired or endotoxin load is high, it escapes into systemic circulation and triggers widespread inflammation, estrogen elevation, serotonin release, and cortisol production.

Aspirin reduces endotoxin burden through several mechanisms. It improves gut barrier integrity by reducing gut inflammation. It supports liver function. It directly antagonizes some of endotoxin's inflammatory signaling pathways. The reduction in endotoxin translates to reduced systemic inflammation, lower estrogen recirculation (endotoxin impairs hepatic estrogen clearance), and lower serotonin (endotoxin triggers intestinal serotonin release).

## Anti-Cancer Properties

The epidemiological evidence for aspirin's anti-cancer effects is substantial and consistent. Regular aspirin use is associated with reduced risk of colorectal cancer (the most robust data), as well as reduced risk of breast, prostate, lung, and esophageal cancers. Meta-analyses suggest approximately 20-30% reduction in colorectal cancer incidence and mortality among regular aspirin users.

The mechanisms are multiple:
- **COX-2 inhibition** reduces PGE2, which promotes tumor angiogenesis, suppresses immune surveillance, and promotes cancer cell survival
- **Anti-platelet effects** may reduce the ability of circulating tumor cells to use platelet "cloaking" to evade immune detection and establish metastases
- **Anti-estrogenic effects** reduce estrogen-driven proliferation
- **Anti-serotonin effects** reduce serotonin's growth-promoting effects on various tumors
- **Improved mitochondrial function** opposes the Warburg-type glycolytic metabolism that cancer cells depend on
- **Reduced endotoxin** lowers the chronic inflammatory milieu that promotes tumor initiation and progression

Despite this data, aspirin is rarely recommended for cancer prevention by mainstream oncology, in part because there is no commercial incentive to promote it.

## How to Use Aspirin

Peat's approach to aspirin was practical and straightforward:

**Dose.** Low doses of 75-100mg are effective for many purposes. For acute anti-inflammatory needs, higher doses (300-500mg) can be used. Peat generally recommended lower doses taken regularly rather than occasional high doses.

**With food.** Always take aspirin with food, preferably with a meal containing some fat and protein. This reduces gastric irritation and improves absorption. Taking aspirin on an empty stomach increases the risk of the gastric side effects that have been used to scare people away from it.

**Timing.** With meals, once or twice daily. Some take it with their largest meal.

**Form.** Plain aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) is fine. Enteric-coated aspirin is marketed as "gentler on the stomach" but actually delivers the aspirin to the small intestine where it can cause more serious damage than in the stomach. Buffered aspirin is acceptable. Dissolving aspirin in warm water or taking it with baking soda can reduce gastric irritation.

## The Vitamin K Connection

Aspirin can increase the body's requirement for vitamin K. This is because aspirin's anti-clotting effects are partly mediated through vitamin K-dependent clotting factors, and chronic aspirin use may deplete vitamin K stores over time.

The practical implication is that regular aspirin users should ensure adequate vitamin K intake. Vitamin K1 is abundant in green leafy vegetables. Vitamin K2 (MK-4) is found in animal fats, egg yolks, and full-fat dairy. Peat noted that vitamin K2 in particular has anti-inflammatory and anti-cancer properties of its own, making it a natural complement to aspirin therapy.

The concern about aspirin and bleeding risk is real but overblown. At low doses (100mg or less), with food, and with adequate vitamin K, the bleeding risk is minimal for most people. The populations at genuine risk -- those with existing bleeding disorders, active ulcers, or on anticoagulant drugs -- are identifiable and can be counseled accordingly.

## Why Peat Preferred Aspirin Over Other NSAIDs

Several features distinguish aspirin from other nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs:

**Irreversible COX inhibition.** Aspirin acetylates the COX enzyme permanently, meaning the inhibition lasts for the lifetime of the enzyme (or the platelet, in the case of COX-1 in platelets). This produces more consistent effects than the reversible inhibition of ibuprofen or naproxen.

**Anti-serotonin effects.** Other NSAIDs do not share aspirin's anti-serotonin properties to the same degree.

**Mitochondrial support.** While other NSAIDs are primarily anti-inflammatory, aspirin has unique pro-metabolic effects that other NSAIDs lack or even oppose.

**Safety profile.** Despite the narrative, aspirin's long-term safety profile is better established and more favorable than newer NSAIDs, many of which increase cardiovascular risk (as the Vioxx disaster demonstrated).

**Cost and availability.** Aspirin is essentially free compared to prescription anti-inflammatories, which means it is accessible to anyone regardless of insurance or income.

## The Broader Context

Aspirin exemplifies a recurring pattern in medicine: a safe, cheap, effective treatment is marginalized in favor of expensive, patented alternatives that are no more effective and often less safe. The medical establishment's ambivalence toward aspirin -- acknowledging its benefits in cardiovascular prevention while failing to recommend it broadly for its anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory, and metabolic benefits -- reflects the structural incentives of a system driven by pharmaceutical profit rather than patient outcomes.

For the individual, the practical conclusion is simple. Aspirin at low doses, taken with meals, is one of the most cost-effective health interventions available. It reduces inflammation, lowers serotonin, opposes estrogen, supports mitochondrial function, reduces endotoxin burden, and lowers cancer risk. Its risks are manageable and well-understood. Its benefits are broad and well-documented. It costs almost nothing.

That a medicine this good exists and is actively discouraged tells you something important about the system dispensing medical advice.
