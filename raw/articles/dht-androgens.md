# DHT and Androgens: The Real Masculine Hormone

## The Androgen Hierarchy

Testosterone gets all the attention. It is the hormone people think of when they think of masculinity, strength, and vitality. But testosterone is, in many ways, an intermediate — a precursor to the androgen that actually does most of the heavy lifting at the cellular level.

**Dihydrotestosterone (DHT)** is 3 to 5 times more potent than testosterone at the androgen receptor. It binds more tightly, dissociates more slowly, and produces stronger transcriptional effects. DHT is what drives the androgenic functions that people attribute to testosterone. Understanding this distinction — and understanding why modern medicine has waged war on DHT — is critical to Peat's framework on hormones and health.

## What DHT Does

DHT acts through the androgen receptor in tissues throughout the body. Its effects are specific and measurable:

**Neurological function.** DHT is a neurosteroid. It promotes assertiveness, confidence, decisiveness, and spatial reasoning. Men with adequate DHT report greater mental clarity, stronger sense of purpose, and reduced anxiety. DHT has direct anti-anxiety effects — it is a positive allosteric modulator at the GABA-A receptor via its metabolites (3-alpha-androstanediol), producing a calming effect similar to progesterone's metabolite allopregnanolone.

**Sexual function.** DHT is the primary androgen responsible for libido and erectile function in men. Penile tissue is highly dependent on DHT for maintaining sensitivity and vascular function. Testosterone alone, without conversion to DHT, does not fully support sexual function — this is why men on 5-alpha reductase inhibitors experience sexual dysfunction even when testosterone levels remain normal.

**Bone density.** DHT promotes osteoblast activity and bone mineralization. The androgen receptor in bone responds more strongly to DHT than to testosterone.

**Muscle maintenance.** While testosterone is the primary driver of skeletal muscle hypertrophy, DHT contributes to strength and neuromuscular function. DHT also has anti-catabolic effects, opposing cortisol's muscle-wasting action.

**Prostate health.** This is where the mainstream narrative goes wrong, and we will address it directly below.

**Skin and sebaceous function.** DHT regulates sebaceous gland activity. Adequate DHT maintains healthy skin turnover and wound healing. Excessive DHT relative to other factors can contribute to acne in susceptible individuals, but this is a problem of imbalance rather than DHT itself being harmful.

**Voice and facial structure.** DHT drives the masculinization of facial structure during puberty and maintains vocal cord thickness. The deep voice and strong jawline associated with high androgens are DHT-mediated.

## The 5-Alpha Reductase Pathway

Testosterone is converted to DHT by the enzyme **5-alpha reductase (5AR)**, which exists in three isoforms:

- **Type 1** — found in skin, liver, and brain
- **Type 2** — found in prostate, seminal vesicles, hair follicles, and genital skin
- **Type 3** — more recently discovered, broadly distributed

This conversion is not a side reaction or an accident. It is a critical metabolic step that amplifies the androgenic signal in tissues that need it. The body expresses 5AR in specific tissues precisely because those tissues require the stronger androgenic effect of DHT.

**Thyroid hormone upregulates 5-alpha reductase.** This is one of the most important connections in the Peat framework. Adequate T3 increases 5AR activity, which increases DHT production. This is why hypothyroid men often present with symptoms of androgen deficiency — not because testosterone is low, but because conversion to the active androgen is impaired.

The chain runs: Thyroid (T3) --> 5-alpha reductase activity --> Testosterone conversion to DHT --> Androgenic effects

Fixing thyroid function often resolves apparent androgen deficiency without any direct testosterone intervention.

## Finasteride and Post-Finasteride Syndrome

**Finasteride** (brand names Propecia, Proscar) is a 5-alpha reductase inhibitor prescribed for two conditions: male pattern hair loss and benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). It blocks the conversion of testosterone to DHT, typically reducing serum DHT by 60-70%.

The drug was marketed as safe and well-tolerated. The reality, documented by thousands of patients and a growing body of research, is far more disturbing.

**Post-Finasteride Syndrome (PFS)** is characterized by:

- **Persistent sexual dysfunction** — reduced libido, erectile dysfunction, loss of genital sensitivity, reduced ejaculate volume. In many cases, these symptoms persist for months, years, or permanently after discontinuing the drug.
- **Cognitive impairment** — brain fog, difficulty concentrating, memory problems, slowed thinking. DHT and its metabolites have direct neuroprotective and neuroactive effects. Blocking their production impairs brain function.
- **Depression and anhedonia** — loss of pleasure, emotional flatness, suicidal ideation. Multiple suicides have been reported among PFS sufferers.
- **Physical changes** — loss of muscle mass, increased body fat, gynecomastia (breast tissue growth from unopposed estrogen), penile tissue changes including fibrosis.
- **Anxiety and panic** — loss of the anxiolytic neurosteroids derived from DHT (3-alpha-androstanediol and its GABA-modulating effects).

The mechanism is clear: by blocking 5AR, finasteride does not just reduce DHT. It disrupts the entire cascade of neurosteroid production that depends on 5-alpha reduction. The neurosteroid allopregnanolone (derived from progesterone via 5AR) is also reduced, contributing to the anxiety and depression.

There is evidence that finasteride may cause epigenetic changes — alterations in gene expression that persist after the drug is discontinued. This could explain why some men never recover.

Peat viewed finasteride as a catastrophically misguided drug — one that attacks a fundamental metabolic pathway based on a flawed understanding of prostate biology.

## The Prostate Myth: Estrogen, Not DHT

The mainstream rationale for blocking DHT is the belief that DHT drives prostate enlargement (BPH) and prostate cancer. This belief is the basis for prescribing finasteride and for viewing DHT as inherently dangerous.

Peat argued, with substantial evidence, that this narrative is wrong. **Estrogen, not DHT, is the primary driver of prostate pathology.**

The evidence:

- **Young men have the highest DHT levels and virtually no prostate problems.** If DHT caused prostate disease, it should peak in youth. Instead, prostate issues emerge in older men as DHT declines and estrogen rises.
- **Estrogen receptors in the prostate** drive proliferation. Estrogen promotes stromal growth and inflammation in the prostate — the hallmarks of BPH.
- **Aromatase activity increases with age.** As men age, more testosterone is converted to estrogen (via aromatase, upregulated by increasing body fat and declining thyroid function). The ratio shifts from androgen-dominant to estrogen-dominant.
- **Men with higher DHT do not have higher prostate cancer rates** in well-designed studies. The correlation between DHT and prostate cancer was assumed based on the observation that castration (removing all androgens) causes prostate cancer to regress. But this does not mean that normal DHT levels cause cancer — it means that cancer cells, like all cells, need some hormonal support to survive.
- **Estrogen and prolactin promote prostate cancer progression.** These are the hormones that rise when metabolic function declines, and they are the ones associated with aggressive prostate disease.

The anti-DHT paradigm in prostate medicine is, in Peat's view, a case of treating the wrong target — blocking a protective hormone while ignoring the actual cause.

## Hair Loss: A More Complex Picture

Male pattern baldness is attributed to DHT's effect on hair follicles — specifically, DHT miniaturizes follicles in genetically susceptible areas (temples, crown). This is the basis for finasteride prescriptions for hair loss.

Peat acknowledged the local role of DHT in hair follicle regression but framed it differently:

- **Scalp-specific inflammation** plays a significant role in hair loss. Prostaglandin D2 (PGD2), which is elevated in balding scalp tissue, is produced from arachidonic acid — a PUFA. Reducing PUFA intake may reduce PGD2-mediated follicle miniaturization.
- **Hypothyroidism causes hair loss** independent of androgens, and many men with "androgenic" hair loss are also hypothyroid.
- **The tradeoff of blocking DHT systemically** (sexual dysfunction, depression, cognitive impairment, increased estrogen) to preserve hair is, in Peat's calculus, obviously unfavorable. You are sacrificing the neurosteroid environment your brain and body depend on for a cosmetic concern.
- **Topical anti-androgens** applied only to the scalp are far less destructive than systemic 5AR inhibition, though even these are not without risk.

## How to Support DHT Production Naturally

The goal is not to artificially elevate DHT but to ensure the body's natural conversion pathway operates without obstruction:

**Optimize thyroid function.** T3 directly upregulates 5-alpha reductase. This is the most impactful lever. If thyroid function is low, DHT production will be impaired regardless of testosterone levels. Monitor basal temperature and pulse. Address thyroid before reaching for androgen supplements.

**Reduce PUFA intake.** Polyunsaturated fats suppress thyroid function (indirectly reducing 5AR activity) and promote the inflammatory prostaglandins that oppose androgen signaling. Seed oil avoidance supports the entire androgen cascade.

**Reduce estrogen.** Estrogen opposes androgens at multiple levels — it increases SHBG (binding testosterone), promotes aromatase (converting testosterone to estrogen), and directly antagonizes androgen receptor signaling. The strategies outlined in the estrogen dominance article apply here: carrot salad, liver support, weight management, xenoestrogen avoidance.

**Adequate zinc.** Zinc is required for testosterone synthesis, inhibits aromatase, and supports 5AR activity. Oysters, red meat, and pumpkin seeds are good dietary sources.

**Adequate saturated fat and cholesterol.** Steroid hormones are synthesized from cholesterol. Extremely low-fat diets reduce testosterone and its downstream metabolites. Eggs, butter, and animal fats provide the substrate.

**Resistance training.** Acute resistance exercise increases testosterone and DHT transiently. Chronic training improves androgen receptor density and sensitivity.

**Manage stress.** Cortisol and testosterone compete for precursors (pregnenolone). Chronic stress diverts pregnenolone toward cortisol production and away from the androgen pathway. Cortisol also directly suppresses gonadotropin release.

**Adequate vitamin A (retinol).** Required for steroid hormone synthesis and receptor function. Liver is the best dietary source.

## The Broader Point

The war on DHT — through finasteride, dutasteride, and the cultural narrative that DHT is a harmful hormone that causes hair loss and prostate disease — is a war on masculine vitality, fought on the basis of incomplete science.

DHT is what makes the androgen signal functional. It is what converts testosterone from a circulating number on a lab report into lived effects: confidence, clarity, sexual function, bone strength, neurological resilience. Blocking it produces a predictable syndrome of diminished function across every domain it supports.

The Peat framework sees DHT not as a problem to be managed but as a marker of healthy metabolism — the downstream product of good thyroid function, adequate nutrition, and low estrogenic burden. When the system works, DHT is produced in appropriate amounts and does its job. When the system is compromised — by PUFAs, hypothyroidism, estrogen excess, and stress — DHT drops, and the symptoms of its absence are treated as separate diseases rather than as manifestations of a single metabolic problem.

Support the system. The hormones follow.
