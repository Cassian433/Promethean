# Estrogen Dominance: The Stress Hormone Hiding in Plain Sight

## Reframing Estrogen

The conventional narrative is that estrogen is "the female hormone" — essential, protective, and something women need more of as they age. This narrative is not just incomplete. It is dangerously wrong, and understanding why is one of the most consequential pieces of the Peat framework.

Estrogen is a stress hormone. It promotes water retention, inflammation, histamine release, blood clotting, fat deposition, and cellular proliferation. In excess — which is the default state in the modern environment — it suppresses thyroid function, opposes progesterone, raises prolactin, and drives a constellation of symptoms that affect both women and men.

Ray Peat was writing about estrogen's harmful effects decades before the mainstream began to acknowledge problems with hormone replacement therapy. His position was clear: the primary goal is not to supplement estrogen but to reduce it and support its metabolic opponent, progesterone.

## What Estrogen Actually Does

In appropriate amounts, during specific phases of the menstrual cycle, estrogen serves reproductive functions. It promotes endometrial growth and prepares the body for potential pregnancy. The problems begin when estrogen is chronically elevated relative to progesterone — a condition that is now the rule rather than the exception.

Chronic estrogen excess promotes:

**Water retention and edema.** Estrogen increases capillary permeability, allowing fluid to leak into tissues. The bloating, puffiness, and weight fluctuation many women experience premenstrually is estrogen-driven. In men, this manifests as subcutaneous water retention and a soft, puffy appearance.

**Histamine release.** Estrogen stimulates mast cells to release histamine, and histamine stimulates the ovaries to produce more estrogen. This creates a vicious cycle. Allergies, hives, nasal congestion, and histamine intolerance are often estrogen-related problems.

**Inflammation.** Estrogen activates NF-kB and other inflammatory signaling pathways. It promotes the production of inflammatory prostaglandins and cytokines. Autoimmune conditions, which disproportionately affect women, are strongly associated with estrogen dominance.

**Prolactin elevation.** Estrogen stimulates prolactin secretion from the pituitary. Elevated prolactin suppresses gonadotropins (LH and FSH), reduces testosterone production in men, disrupts menstrual cycles in women, and promotes breast tissue growth in both sexes. It also causes emotional flatness and reduced motivation.

**Thyroid suppression.** Estrogen increases thyroid-binding globulin (TBG), sequestering thyroid hormone in the blood and making it unavailable to tissues. A woman with rising estrogen can have normal total thyroid levels on blood work while experiencing profound hypothyroid symptoms.

**SHBG elevation and testosterone binding.** Estrogen increases sex hormone-binding globulin, which binds testosterone and makes it biologically inactive. This is one mechanism by which estrogen-dominant men have low free testosterone despite normal total testosterone.

**Cellular proliferation.** Estrogen promotes cell division. This is its reproductive function — growing the endometrial lining — but in excess it drives proliferation in tissues where growth is unwanted: breast tissue, uterine fibroids, endometriosis, and estrogen-receptor-positive cancers.

## Aromatase: The Conversion Problem

Aromatase is the enzyme that converts androgens (testosterone, androstenedione) into estrogens. It is present in fat tissue, the brain, bone, and other sites. The more body fat a person carries, the more aromatase activity they have, and the more testosterone is converted to estrogen.

This creates a self-reinforcing cycle in overweight men:

1. Excess body fat increases aromatase activity
2. More testosterone is converted to estrogen
3. Estrogen promotes further fat deposition
4. More fat means more aromatase
5. Free testosterone drops, estrogen rises further

The result is the increasingly common phenotype: overweight men with gynecomastia, low libido, emotional lability, and measurably low testosterone. The root cause is not testosterone deficiency — it is estrogen excess driven by aromatase.

Factors that increase aromatase activity: obesity, alcohol, insulin resistance, inflammation, and aging. Factors that inhibit it: adequate zinc, progesterone, certain flavonoids, and low body fat.

## Xenoestrogens: The Environmental Assault

Modern humans are exposed to synthetic chemicals that mimic estrogen at the receptor level. These xenoestrogens are pervasive:

- **BPA and BPS** (plastic liners in cans, receipts, food containers)
- **Phthalates** (fragrances, soft plastics, personal care products)
- **Parabens** (preservatives in cosmetics and shampoos)
- **Atrazine** (herbicide, found in water supply — documented to feminize frogs)
- **PCBs and dioxins** (persistent environmental pollutants)
- **Soy isoflavones** (genistein and daidzein are phytoestrogens)

These compounds bind estrogen receptors and activate estrogenic signaling even in small doses. The "dose makes the poison" model does not apply well to endocrine disruptors — they operate at parts-per-billion concentrations, the same range as natural hormones.

The cumulative effect of xenoestrogen exposure is measurable. Sperm counts in Western men have dropped roughly 50% since the 1970s. Testosterone levels have declined approximately 1% per year since the 1980s. The age of female puberty has dropped significantly. These are population-level effects consistent with widespread estrogenic exposure.

## The Carrot Salad

One of the most distinctive practical recommendations in Peat's work is the daily raw carrot salad. The logic is specific and grounded in physiology:

**Raw carrot fiber is uniquely effective at binding estrogen in the gut.** Estrogen is conjugated in the liver and excreted into the intestine via bile. If gut bacteria possess beta-glucuronidase activity (which many do, especially in dysbiotic guts), they deconjugate the estrogen, allowing it to be reabsorbed into circulation — enterohepatic recirculation.

Raw carrot fiber, due to its particular structure, resists bacterial fermentation and physically binds bile-conjugated estrogen and endotoxin, carrying them out in the stool. This reduces the estrogen load that recirculates back to the liver.

The preparation:

- One medium raw carrot, grated or shredded lengthwise
- A small amount of coconut oil (saturated fat aids bile flow)
- A splash of white vinegar (antimicrobial, suppresses gut bacteria)
- Optional: salt

Eaten daily, typically before a meal. The effect is cumulative — it takes days to weeks to notice changes, but many people report reduced bloating, improved bowel regularity, and clearer skin.

This is not a cure for estrogen dominance. It is one tool in a broader strategy of reducing estrogen load.

## Liver Support for Estrogen Clearance

The liver is responsible for metabolizing and clearing estrogen from the body. It does this through two phases of detoxification:

- **Phase 1** (cytochrome P450 enzymes) hydroxylates estrogen
- **Phase 2** (conjugation — glucuronidation, sulfation, methylation) attaches a molecule that makes estrogen water-soluble and excretable

When liver function is impaired — by alcohol, PUFAs, iron overload, or fatty liver disease — estrogen clearance slows and levels rise. Supporting liver function is therefore directly anti-estrogenic.

Factors that support estrogen clearance:

- **Adequate protein intake** — provides amino acids for conjugation reactions (glycine, taurine, methionine)
- **B vitamins** — cofactors for methylation (B6, B12, folate)
- **Adequate thyroid function** — T3 upregulates liver detoxification enzymes
- **Vitamin A (retinol)** — supports Phase 1 enzymes
- **Avoiding alcohol** — competes for the same liver detoxification pathways
- **Reducing PUFA intake** — lipid peroxidation products burden the liver
- **Adequate carbohydrate intake** — the liver requires glycogen to perform detoxification efficiently; low-carb diets impair liver clearance

## Progesterone: The Counter-Hormone

Progesterone is estrogen's physiological opponent. Where estrogen promotes proliferation, water retention, and excitability, progesterone promotes differentiation, diuresis, and calm. The ratio of progesterone to estrogen matters far more than the absolute level of either hormone.

Progesterone:

- Opposes estrogen at the receptor level
- Reduces water retention
- Stabilizes the nervous system (it is a GABA agonist via its metabolite allopregnanolone)
- Supports thyroid function by reducing TBG
- Is anti-inflammatory
- Protects against estrogen-driven proliferation

In premenopausal women, progesterone is produced primarily after ovulation by the corpus luteum. Anovulatory cycles — increasingly common due to stress, PUFA consumption, and endocrine disruptors — produce little or no progesterone, leaving estrogen unopposed for the entire cycle.

In men, progesterone is produced by the adrenals and testes in smaller amounts. It serves as a precursor to cortisol and testosterone and has direct anti-estrogenic effects.

## Why Modern Men Have Rising Estrogen

The convergence of several factors has created an epidemic of estrogen dominance in men:

1. **Seed oil consumption** — PUFAs suppress thyroid, and low thyroid reduces liver clearance of estrogen
2. **Obesity** — more aromatase, more conversion of testosterone to estrogen
3. **Xenoestrogen exposure** — plastics, pesticides, personal care products
4. **Alcohol** — directly increases aromatase and impairs liver clearance
5. **Sedentary lifestyle** — reduces metabolic rate, promotes fat gain
6. **Chronic stress** — cortisol steals pregnenolone from the progesterone pathway (pregnenolone steal), and stress promotes fat storage
7. **Low-protein diets** — inadequate amino acids for liver conjugation

The solution is not testosterone replacement therapy (though it has its place). The solution is to address the estrogen excess: reduce PUFA intake, reduce body fat, minimize xenoestrogen exposure, support liver function, and optimize thyroid. When estrogen drops and thyroid function improves, testosterone and DHT often normalize without direct supplementation.

Estrogen is not evil. It is a powerful hormone that serves specific reproductive functions. But in the modern environment, it is chronically elevated in nearly everyone, and the consequences — from infertility to cancer to the quiet erosion of vitality — are everywhere if you know what to look for.
