# Lab Interpretation Through the Peat Lens

Standard medical lab interpretation relies on reference ranges derived from population averages. Ray Peat's fundamental objection to this approach was simple: the average person in a modern industrial society is not healthy. Reference ranges based on sick populations define mediocrity as normal. A TSH of 3.5 is "within range" because most people in the reference population have suboptimal thyroid function. That does not make it optimal.

Peat's lab framework interprets blood work through the lens of metabolic rate: what do the numbers tell us about the rate and efficiency of mitochondrial energy production, the balance between protective and stress hormones, and the presence of metabolic inhibitors? This reframing changes the interpretation of nearly every common lab marker.

## Thyroid Panel

### TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)

Mainstream medicine considers TSH up to 4.0-4.5 mIU/L as normal. Some endocrinologists will not treat until TSH exceeds 10.0. This is one of the starkest disagreements between conventional medicine and the Peat framework.

In the Peat framework, optimal TSH is below 1.5 mIU/L, with many practitioners targeting below 1.0. TSH is a pituitary hormone that rises when the pituitary detects insufficient thyroid hormone. A TSH of 3.0 means the pituitary is working harder to stimulate the thyroid — it is compensation for inadequate output, not a sign that everything is fine.

Elevated TSH also has direct harmful effects beyond indicating thyroid insufficiency. TSH stimulates aromatase (the enzyme that converts testosterone to estrogen), promotes thyroid nodule growth, and is associated with increased cardiovascular risk even within the "normal" range.

### Free T3

Free T3 is the active thyroid hormone — the one that actually enters cells and increases metabolic rate. Peat framework targets the upper third of the reference range. If the range is 2.0-4.4 pg/mL, optimal is roughly 3.5-4.4. Many people with "normal" thyroid function have free T3 in the lower half of the range, which correlates with lower metabolic rate, higher cholesterol (thyroid hormone drives cholesterol conversion to steroid hormones), and more symptoms of hypothyroidism.

Free T3 should be interpreted alongside temperature and pulse. A free T3 in the upper third of range with a waking temperature of 97.0F suggests thyroid hormone resistance rather than adequate thyroid function.

### Free T4

Free T4 is the storage form of thyroid hormone, requiring conversion to T3 for biological activity. A high free T4 with low free T3 suggests impaired conversion — the deiodinase enzymes that convert T4 to T3 are being inhibited. Common causes: high cortisol, selenium deficiency, iron dysregulation, and PUFA-mediated suppression.

### Reverse T3

Reverse T3 (rT3) is an inactive metabolite of T4 produced when the body is shunting thyroid hormone away from the active T3 pathway. Elevated rT3 indicates thyroid hormone resistance or excessive stress-mediated inactivation. The ratio of free T3 to reverse T3 is more informative than either value alone. A ratio below 20 (when fT3 is in pg/mL and rT3 in ng/dL) suggests functional hypothyroidism regardless of what TSH says.

Common causes of elevated rT3: chronic stress (cortisol promotes rT3 production), caloric restriction, inflammation, and iron overload.

## Sex Hormones

### Estradiol (in Men)

Mainstream medicine rarely tests estradiol in men, and when it does, the reference range extends up to 40-60 pg/mL. Peat's framework considers lower estradiol better, targeting below 20-25 pg/mL in men.

Estradiol in men promotes fat storage (especially abdominal and chest), suppresses testosterone production via negative feedback, promotes prostate growth, increases clotting risk, and drives water retention. It is produced by aromatase, which is concentrated in fat tissue — creating a vicious cycle where estrogen promotes fat gain, and fat tissue produces more estrogen.

Elevated estradiol in men correlates with TSH elevation (TSH stimulates aromatase), low zinc, high body fat, alcohol consumption, and high PUFA intake (PUFAs promote aromatase activity).

### Prolactin

Prolactin should be low in both men and women (when not pregnant or breastfeeding). The Peat framework targets below 8 ng/mL in men and below 15 ng/mL in non-pregnant women, though lower is generally better.

Elevated prolactin suppresses gonadotropins (reducing testosterone in men and progesterone in women), promotes tumor growth (prolactin receptors are found on many cancer types), impairs immune function, and is associated with anxiety and depression. Common causes of elevated prolactin: estrogen excess, serotonin excess, hypothyroidism, and certain medications (SSRIs, antipsychotics).

Vitamin E, dopamine-supporting strategies (bright light, adequate protein), and thyroid optimization all help lower prolactin.

### Testosterone and DHT

Total testosterone, free testosterone, and DHT (dihydrotestosterone) together give a picture of androgenic status. Peat valued DHT particularly — it is the most potent androgen, is anti-estrogenic, and supports brain function. Mainstream medicine has increasingly pathologized DHT (linking it to hair loss and prostate issues), but Peat saw adequate DHT as protective.

Low free testosterone with high SHBG suggests estrogen dominance (estrogen stimulates SHBG production, which binds testosterone, making it unavailable). The goal is adequate free testosterone with proportionally adequate DHT, indicating healthy 5-alpha-reductase activity.

### SHBG (Sex Hormone Binding Globulin)

SHBG binds sex hormones in the blood, making them unavailable for tissue uptake. High SHBG effectively reduces free testosterone, free DHT, and free estradiol (though its effect on estradiol is less pronounced because estradiol has other binding proteins).

Elevated SHBG is driven by estrogen, hyperthyroidism, and liver stress. Low SHBG can indicate insulin resistance or hypothyroidism. In the Peat framework, moderately low SHBG with good free testosterone levels is generally preferable to high SHBG that reduces androgen availability.

## Ferritin and Iron

### Ferritin

This is one of the most important markers in the Peat framework and one where mainstream and Peat interpretations diverge most dramatically. Mainstream medicine considers ferritin up to 300-400 ng/mL as normal in men. Some labs flag it only above 500.

Peat considered iron accumulation one of the primary drivers of aging and degenerative disease. Free iron catalyzes the Fenton reaction, generating hydroxyl radicals — the most destructive reactive oxygen species. These radicals attack PUFAs in cell membranes, initiating lipid peroxidation cascades. The combination of high iron and high tissue PUFA content creates a self-reinforcing cycle of oxidative damage.

The Peat framework targets ferritin of 40-80 ng/mL. Below 40 may indicate genuine deficiency (especially in menstruating women). Above 80-100 suggests iron accumulation that is contributing to oxidative stress. Men and postmenopausal women are particularly susceptible because they lack the regular iron loss that menstruation provides.

Interventions for high ferritin: blood donation (the most effective), avoiding iron-fortified foods (cereals, breads), reducing red meat if intake is very high, and ensuring adequate copper (via ceruloplasmin, copper makes iron functional and prevents its free accumulation).

## Cholesterol

### Total Cholesterol

Peat did not fear high total cholesterol. Cholesterol is the raw material for all steroid hormones — pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, cortisol, testosterone, estradiol, and vitamin D. Low total cholesterol (below 160-170 mg/dL) is actually concerning in the Peat framework because it suggests either insufficient production (liver dysfunction, inadequate thyroid function) or excessive conversion (high cortisol demand consuming cholesterol).

High total cholesterol in the context of hypothyroidism is expected and is a sign that thyroid function needs support, not that statins are needed. Thyroid hormone drives the conversion of cholesterol into steroid hormones and bile acids. When thyroid is low, cholesterol accumulates because it is not being used. The correct treatment is thyroid support, not cholesterol-lowering drugs.

### LDL

LDL rises in hypothyroidism for the same reason total cholesterol does — reduced clearance due to inadequate thyroid hormone. Peat framework practitioners look at LDL particle oxidation (driven by PUFAs and iron) as the real risk factor, not LDL quantity. A person with high LDL composed of large, buoyant particles (low PUFA, low oxidative stress) is at much lower risk than someone with lower LDL composed of small, dense, oxidized particles.

## Vitamin D (25-OH)

The Peat framework targets 40-60 ng/mL. Below 30 is deficient. Above 80-100 may indicate excessive supplementation without adequate K2 and magnesium, potentially leading to calcium dysregulation. Vitamin D status reflects both sun exposure and supplementation and directly impacts calcium metabolism, immune function, and gene expression.

## Glucose and Insulin

Fasting glucose in the Peat framework is viewed differently than in mainstream medicine. A fasting glucose of 85-100 mg/dL is considered healthy — the body is maintaining adequate fuel supply. Very low fasting glucose (below 75) may indicate excessive insulin sensitivity or cortisol-driven glucose management (where glucose appears low because cortisol is constantly mobilizing it for emergency use).

Fasting insulin should be low (below 5-8 mIU/L), indicating that the body is efficiently managing glucose without excessive insulin secretion.

## Temperature and Pulse: The Free Tests

Peat considered waking body temperature and resting pulse rate the most informative "labs" available — and they are free. Waking oral temperature should be 97.8-98.6F, rising to 98.6F or higher by mid-morning after eating. Resting pulse should be 75-85 bpm in a relaxed state.

Low temperature with low pulse indicates hypothyroidism — the metabolic rate is genuinely suppressed. Low temperature with high pulse suggests adrenaline compensation — the body is using stress hormones to compensate for inadequate metabolic capacity. High temperature with high pulse in a relaxed state indicates good metabolic function.

These measurements, tracked daily, provide more real-time metabolic information than any blood test. They respond to dietary changes, thyroid supplementation, sleep quality, and stress levels within hours to days, while blood markers may take weeks to shift.

## The Interpretive Framework

The key principle: lab values are not isolated numbers. They form a pattern that reveals the underlying metabolic state. High TSH + high cholesterol + high ferritin + high estradiol in a man tells a clear story: hypothyroidism with iron accumulation and estrogen dominance. Each value confirms and contextualizes the others. The Peat framework reads labs as an integrated metabolic narrative, not as a checklist of individual pass/fail values.
