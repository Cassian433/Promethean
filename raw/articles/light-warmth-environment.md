# Light, Warmth, and Environment: The Metabolic Context

Ray Peat understood that metabolism does not happen in a vacuum. The organism exists within an environment, and the quality of that environment — its light, temperature, and electromagnetic characteristics — directly shapes the rate and efficiency of cellular energy production. This is not a minor detail. For Peat, environmental factors were as important as diet and hormones in determining whether a person's cells produced energy efficiently or slid toward the stress-dominated state he associated with disease and aging.

## Red and Infrared Light: Feeding the Mitochondria Directly

The most striking claim in Peat's environmental framework is that light — specifically red and near-infrared wavelengths — directly stimulates cellular respiration. This is not metaphorical. Cytochrome c oxidase, also known as Complex IV in the mitochondrial electron transport chain, is a chromoprotein. It absorbs light in the red and near-infrared spectrum (roughly 600-900 nm). When it absorbs these photons, the enzyme's activity increases. More electrons flow through the chain. More oxygen is consumed. More ATP and CO2 are produced.

This is the mechanism behind photobiomodulation, sometimes called low-level laser therapy or red light therapy. Peat was discussing this decades before it became a consumer wellness trend. He pointed to the work of Tiina Karu, a Russian photobiologist who demonstrated that red and near-infrared light could rescue cells from metabolic inhibition. The practical implication is straightforward: exposure to these wavelengths of light is a direct metabolic stimulus, operating at the deepest level of energy production.

Incandescent light bulbs, which Peat preferred, emit a broad spectrum rich in red and infrared wavelengths. Fluorescent and LED lights, by contrast, are heavily weighted toward blue wavelengths and produce very little infrared. Peat considered this a genuine health concern — not because of some vague notion of "natural" light, but because the spectral composition of your lighting environment either supports or fails to support mitochondrial function.

## Bright Light, Serotonin, and Melatonin

Peat's views on serotonin and melatonin placed him in sharp opposition to mainstream thinking. Where conventional medicine treats serotonin as the "feel-good neurotransmitter" and melatonin as a beneficial sleep hormone, Peat saw both as stress substances associated with metabolic suppression.

Bright light during the day suppresses serotonin synthesis in the brain and inhibits melatonin production from the pineal gland. Peat considered this suppressive effect beneficial. His reasoning: serotonin promotes inflammation, gut disturbance, vasoconstriction, learned helplessness, and fibrosis. Melatonin, rather than being a restorative molecule, is produced in response to darkness as part of the organism's stress-adaptation to reduced energy availability. It suppresses thyroid function and reproductive hormones.

In contrast, bright daytime light stimulates dopamine production. Dopamine is associated with motivation, alertness, coordination, and a generally higher metabolic state. The practical advice flows directly: get substantial bright light exposure during the day. Peat often mentioned that people in tropical and equatorial regions, who receive intense sunlight year-round, tend to have higher metabolic rates, later onset of degenerative diseases, and different hormonal profiles than people living in dim northern climates.

## Seasonal Affective Disorder as Metabolic Truth

Seasonal affective disorder (SAD) is usually framed as a mood disorder treatable with SSRIs or light boxes. Peat reframed it as a real metabolic event. In winter, reduced light exposure leads to increased serotonin and melatonin, suppressed thyroid function, lower dopamine, and a shift toward stress-hormone dominance. The depression and fatigue people experience are not psychiatric symptoms requiring pharmaceutical intervention — they are the subjective experience of suppressed oxidative metabolism.

This is why bright light therapy actually works for SAD, and it is also why Peat recommended living in warmer, sunnier climates when possible. The effect is not merely psychological. Light exposure changes hormone production, neurotransmitter balance, and the rate at which every cell in the body produces energy.

## Warmth Supports Metabolism; Cold Suppresses It

Peat was unambiguous: warmth is pro-metabolic, cold is anti-metabolic. This put him squarely against the cold-exposure enthusiasts who promote ice baths, cold plunges, and cryotherapy as health interventions.

The reasoning is physiological. When the body is exposed to cold, it must mount a stress response to maintain core temperature. This involves adrenaline, cortisol, and other stress hormones. The initial feeling of alertness and euphoria from cold exposure is not evidence of benefit — it is the sensation of a stress response. Adrenaline makes you feel awake and sharp in the short term, just as it does during a car accident. That does not make car accidents therapeutic.

Chronic cold exposure suppresses thyroid function. The body downregulates its metabolic rate to conserve heat. Peripheral circulation decreases. Reproductive function is impaired (cold environments are associated with lower fertility across species). The metabolic suppression that cold induces is the exact opposite of what Peat's framework aims to achieve.

Warmth, on the other hand, supports enzyme function (enzymes work faster at higher temperatures within the physiological range), promotes peripheral circulation, reduces the need for stress-hormone-mediated thermogenesis, and allows the body to direct its energy toward repair, growth, and reproduction rather than survival.

## Practical Applications: Warm Baths and Ambient Temperature

Peat recommended warm baths as a simple, effective intervention. A warm bath raises core body temperature, reduces cortisol, promotes vasodilation, and can improve sleep quality. He suggested that people who feel cold, who have low basal temperatures, or who are in a hypothyroid state should actively keep themselves warm rather than "toughening up" with cold exposure.

Maintaining a warm ambient environment matters too. Peat pointed out that hospitals keep patients warm because hypothermia slows healing. The same principle applies to daily life. Keeping your living and working spaces warm, wearing adequate clothing, and avoiding unnecessary cold exposure are all part of supporting the metabolic state.

## Light Exposure and Hormone Production

The relationship between light and hormones extends beyond serotonin and melatonin. Light exposure influences the entire endocrine axis:

- **Thyroid function** improves with adequate light. Seasonal thyroid suppression is well-documented, and Peat connected this to the light-melatonin-thyroid axis.
- **Progesterone** production is supported by bright light and warmth. Peat noted that progesterone levels tend to be higher in summer months.
- **Prolactin**, which Peat associated with stress, tumor promotion, and immune suppression, is suppressed by bright light and dopamine. Darkness and serotonin increase it.
- **Cortisol** patterns are regulated by the light-dark cycle. Disrupted light exposure (shift work, excessive screen time at night) disrupts cortisol rhythm, leading to elevated nighttime cortisol and its downstream metabolic consequences.
- **Vitamin D** synthesis requires UVB exposure on the skin. While Peat was not a sun-avoidance advocate, he emphasized that vitamin D's role is primarily as a hormone regulator of calcium metabolism, connecting light exposure to the calcium-phosphate balance he considered central to cellular function.

## The Integrated View

What distinguishes Peat's environmental framework from typical wellness advice is its mechanistic coherence. He was not saying "sunlight is good for you" as a vague platitude. He was identifying specific molecular targets — cytochrome c oxidase, the serotonin/melatonin axis, dopamine pathways, thyroid hormone cascades — and explaining how the electromagnetic and thermal environment interacts with each one.

The practical takeaway is that optimizing diet and supplements while living in a cold, dimly lit environment with blue-spectrum artificial lighting is working against yourself. The environment is not a minor variable. It is a primary determinant of metabolic rate, hormonal balance, and the trajectory of aging. Bright incandescent or red/infrared light during the day, warmth, and adequate sun exposure form the environmental foundation on which the rest of Peat's framework operates.
