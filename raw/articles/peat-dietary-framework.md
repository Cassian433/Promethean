# The Peat Dietary Framework: What He Actually Ate and Recommended

Ray Peat's dietary recommendations were not arbitrary food preferences or cultural traditions. Every recommendation connected to a specific metabolic rationale — supporting mitochondrial respiration, suppressing stress hormones, providing the raw materials for protective hormones, and avoiding substances that inhibit cellular energy production. Understanding why each food is included (or excluded) makes the framework coherent rather than just a list of rules.

## Orange Juice

Orange juice was arguably the most iconic Peat food, and for good reason. It provides fructose, which is metabolized primarily by the liver. Unlike glucose, which requires insulin for uptake into most tissues, fructose enters liver cells via GLUT5 transporters independently of insulin. The liver converts fructose into glucose and glycogen, maintaining stable blood sugar without the insulin spikes associated with starchy foods.

Orange juice also provides potassium (critical for maintaining the cellular potassium-to-sodium ratio that supports oxidative metabolism), magnesium, and vitamin C. The sugar in orange juice supports thyroid function by lowering cortisol — the body produces less cortisol when adequate glucose is available because cortisol's primary role is to mobilize fuel during scarcity.

Peat recommended fresh-squeezed or high-quality not-from-concentrate orange juice. The sugar content, which mainstream nutrition demonizes, was the point. Sugar is fuel. A cell that has adequate fuel does not need to rely on stress hormones to function.

## Milk

Whole milk, preferably from grass-fed cows, was a dietary staple. Milk provides a balanced combination of protein (casein and whey), fat (mostly saturated and monounsaturated), sugar (lactose, which provides both glucose and galactose), and critically, calcium.

Calcium is central to Peat's framework because it suppresses parathyroid hormone (PTH). When dietary calcium is inadequate, PTH rises to pull calcium from bones. Elevated PTH is inflammatory, promotes calcification of soft tissues (paradoxically), and is associated with increased intracellular calcium — which Peat identified as a key mechanism of cell stress and death. Adequate dietary calcium from milk keeps PTH low, supporting both bone density and cellular function.

Milk also provides a favorable calcium-to-phosphorus ratio. Meat is high in phosphorus relative to calcium, and the imbalance promotes PTH secretion. Combining milk with meat corrects this ratio.

For those who tolerate it, milk was Peat's preferred calcium source over supplements because it comes packaged with the cofactors (vitamin D, vitamin K2, magnesium in grass-fed milk) needed for proper calcium metabolism.

## Coffee

Peat drank coffee regularly and recommended it for its metabolic effects. Coffee raises the metabolic rate, stimulates bile flow (important for clearing estrogen), contains magnesium, and has been shown in epidemiological studies to be associated with lower risk of liver disease, diabetes, and several cancers.

A specific mechanism Peat highlighted: caffeine increases progesterone production. Progesterone is the primary anti-stress, anti-estrogen hormone in his framework. By stimulating progesterone while also providing antioxidants (coffee is one of the highest dietary sources of antioxidants in most people's diets), coffee supports the hormonal balance Peat's framework targets.

Coffee should be consumed with sugar and ideally with milk or cream. Black coffee on an empty stomach can provoke a stress response by lowering blood sugar. With adequate calories, coffee is pro-metabolic. Without them, it becomes another stressor.

## Gelatin and Bone Broth

Gelatin was one of Peat's most therapeutically important food recommendations. The modern diet is heavily weighted toward muscle meat, which is high in the amino acids tryptophan, cysteine, and methionine. These amino acids, in excess relative to glycine, promote inflammation, suppress thyroid function, and drive serotonin synthesis.

Gelatin is primarily composed of glycine, proline, and hydroxyproline. Glycine opposes many of the inflammatory effects of the muscle-meat amino acids. It is anti-inflammatory, supports liver detoxification, improves sleep quality, and helps balance the amino acid profile of a meat-based diet.

Peat recommended consuming gelatin daily — either as powdered gelatin dissolved in juice or coffee, or as bone broth cooked long enough to extract the collagen from bones and connective tissue. The practical goal was to consume roughly equal portions of gelatin-type protein and muscle-meat protein, restoring a ratio more similar to what humans consumed when they ate the whole animal rather than just the lean cuts.

## Coconut Oil

Coconut oil is predominantly saturated fat, including a high proportion of medium-chain triglycerides (MCTs) like lauric acid, capric acid, and caprylic acid. MCTs are absorbed directly into the portal vein and transported to the liver, where they can be oxidized for energy without requiring carnitine transport into mitochondria. This makes coconut oil a uniquely accessible energy source.

Saturated fats do not undergo lipid peroxidation the way PUFAs do. They are chemically stable, producing no malondialdehyde or other toxic aldehydes when metabolized. By replacing seed oils with coconut oil (and butter and tallow), the body's fat stores gradually shift toward a more saturated composition, reducing the ongoing oxidative damage caused by PUFA metabolism.

Peat used coconut oil for cooking and sometimes consumed it directly, mixed into coffee or other foods. It was his primary cooking fat alongside butter.

## Eggs

Eggs provide complete protein with an ideal amino acid profile, fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K2 in pastured eggs), B vitamins, choline, and cholesterol. Peat did not fear dietary cholesterol — he considered it essential. Cholesterol is the precursor to all steroid hormones: pregnenolone, progesterone, DHEA, testosterone, estradiol, cortisol, and aldosterone. Adequate cholesterol intake supports the body's ability to produce these hormones without diverting resources from other metabolic needs.

Eggs from pastured hens have a more favorable fatty acid profile, with lower PUFAs and higher saturated and monounsaturated fats compared to eggs from grain-fed hens. Peat typically recommended two or more eggs daily, cooked in butter or coconut oil.

## Liver

Peat recommended eating liver approximately once per week. Liver is the most nutrient-dense food available, providing preformed retinol (vitamin A), copper, B vitamins (especially B12 and folate), iron (though Peat cautioned about iron excess), and other micronutrients in highly bioavailable forms.

Retinol from liver is particularly important because it works synergistically with thyroid hormone. Vitamin A deficiency impairs thyroid hormone signaling even when thyroid levels are adequate. Copper from liver supports ceruloplasmin, the copper-carrying protein that makes iron bioavailable and prevents its free-radical-generating accumulation.

The once-weekly frequency was deliberate — enough to maintain nutrient stores without excessive retinol or iron intake. Beef or lamb liver were preferred. Peat cautioned against polar bear liver (extreme retinol) and frequent liver consumption beyond once or twice weekly.

## Salt

Peat recommended generous salt intake, which contrasts with mainstream low-sodium guidelines. His reasoning: when sodium intake is low, the body compensates by increasing aldosterone and adrenaline to retain sodium. These are stress hormones. Elevated aldosterone promotes potassium loss, magnesium loss, and inflammation. Elevated adrenaline causes anxiety, sleep disruption, and vasoconstriction.

By consuming adequate salt, you suppress aldosterone and adrenaline, allowing the body to maintain electrolyte balance without stress-hormone involvement. Peat noted that people who salt their food generously tend to sleep better, have lower anxiety, and maintain better blood pressure than those who restrict sodium, provided their overall metabolic function is adequate.

## Shellfish

Shrimp, oysters, and other shellfish provide zinc, selenium, copper, and iodine — trace minerals critical for thyroid function, immune function, and antioxidant enzyme production. Oysters in particular are extremely high in zinc, which supports testosterone production, immune function, and hundreds of enzymatic processes. Selenium is required for the conversion of T4 to T3 (active thyroid hormone) and for glutathione peroxidase activity.

## Well-Cooked Vegetables

Peat was not against vegetables but insisted they be thoroughly cooked. Raw cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, kale, cabbage) contain goitrogens that suppress thyroid function. Raw leafy greens contain oxalates that bind calcium and can contribute to kidney stones. Raw vegetables also contain more fiber, which Peat associated with intestinal irritation and endotoxin production from bacterial fermentation.

Thorough cooking breaks down goitrogens, reduces oxalate content, and softens plant cell walls, making nutrients more bioavailable while reducing anti-nutrients. Root vegetables like potatoes and carrots, squash, and well-cooked greens were all acceptable.

## The Carrot Salad

The raw carrot salad was a specific exception to the cooked-vegetable rule, and it served a unique therapeutic purpose. A daily raw carrot, shredded and dressed with coconut oil, vinegar, and salt, provides a particular type of insoluble fiber that binds estrogen and endotoxin in the gut and carries them out through the stool.

Estrogen is conjugated by the liver and excreted into bile. In the intestine, bacterial enzymes (beta-glucuronidase) can deconjugate it, allowing reabsorption — the enterohepatic circulation of estrogen. The carrot fiber binds the estrogen before bacteria can free it, reducing the total estrogen load. Peat recommended this as a daily practice, especially for anyone dealing with estrogen dominance symptoms.

## Ripe Tropical Fruits

Beyond oranges, Peat favored ripe tropical fruits: mangoes, papayas, watermelon, and grapes. These provide simple sugars (fructose and glucose), potassium, magnesium, and various protective compounds. Ripeness was important — unripe fruit contains more starch, more anti-nutrients, and less sugar. The emphasis on tropical fruits over temperate-climate fruits reflects their higher sugar content and the general principle that environments with more light and warmth produce more metabolically supportive foods.

## The Underlying Logic

Every food in Peat's framework serves at least one of these functions: providing fuel for oxidative metabolism (sugars), providing structural and hormonal raw materials (protein, cholesterol, fat-soluble vitamins), suppressing stress hormones (salt, sugar, calcium), or removing harmful substances (carrot salad, gelatin's glycine). Foods are excluded when they introduce PUFAs, anti-nutrients, or excessive anti-metabolic amino acids. The diet is not restrictive for the sake of restriction — it is precisely targeted at maintaining the highest possible rate of mitochondrial energy production.
