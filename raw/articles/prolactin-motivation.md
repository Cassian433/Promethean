# Prolactin: The "Give Up" Hormone

## Beyond Lactation

Prolactin is known as the hormone that stimulates breast milk production. This is true, but it is like describing cortisol as "the hormone that wakes you up in the morning" -- technically accurate but missing the larger picture. Prolactin is a stress hormone with wide-ranging systemic effects that, when chronically elevated, creates a metabolic and psychological profile that perfectly describes the modern epidemic of unmotivated, anhedonic, emotionally flat individuals who cannot seem to engage with life.

Understanding prolactin and what controls it provides a biochemical explanation for why so many people feel like they are watching their life through glass -- present but unable to participate.

## What Raises Prolactin

Prolactin release is governed by several factors, and nearly all of them are features of modern life:

**Stress.** Any form of stress -- physical, psychological, metabolic -- raises prolactin. This includes sleep deprivation, emotional distress, overexercise, and blood sugar crashes. Prolactin rises as part of the stress response alongside cortisol, but while cortisol gets the attention, prolactin's effects on motivation and mood are arguably more debilitating.

**Estrogen.** Estrogen is the most potent stimulator of prolactin secretion. It acts directly on lactotroph cells in the pituitary gland, increasing both prolactin synthesis and release. This is biologically logical -- estrogen rises during pregnancy to prepare for lactation, and prolactin responds. But in the modern environment of xenoestrogens (from plastics, pesticides, birth control pills in the water supply, and phytoestrogens in soy), chronic estrogen excess chronically elevates prolactin in both women and men.

**Serotonin.** Serotonin stimulates prolactin release through 5-HT2 receptors on lactotroph cells. This is why SSRI antidepressants frequently cause sexual dysfunction, emotional blunting, and weight gain -- all effects consistent with elevated prolactin. The serotonin-prolactin connection also explains why tryptophan-rich meals eaten at night (when serotonin naturally rises) can promote the sleepy, unmotivated state.

**Darkness.** Prolactin rises during darkness and is suppressed by bright light. Seasonal affective disorder, the lethargy and depression of winter months, is partially a prolactin phenomenon. The modern habit of spending days in dim indoor lighting and nights in artificial blue light disrupts this cycle, often keeping prolactin higher than it should be during the day.

**Hypothyroidism.** Low thyroid function raises prolactin through elevated TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone). TRH stimulates both TSH and prolactin release from the pituitary. This is why hypothyroid patients frequently have hyperprolactinemia and its associated symptoms, and why correcting thyroid function often normalizes prolactin without any other intervention.

## What Prolactin Does

When prolactin is chronically elevated beyond its appropriate physiological role, its effects are pervasive:

**Kills libido.** This is prolactin's most noticed effect because it is the most immediately apparent. Elevated prolactin suppresses gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) from the hypothalamus, which reduces LH and FSH, which reduces testosterone and estrogen production from the gonads. In men, this causes erectile dysfunction, low libido, and reduced ejaculate volume. In women, it causes amenorrhea, anovulation, and loss of sexual desire.

**Flattens emotions.** People with hyperprolactinemia describe a characteristic emotional numbness. Not sadness exactly, but an inability to feel strongly about anything. Joy, excitement, anger, desire -- all are muted. This is distinct from classical depression and is often more distressing because the person retains enough awareness to know something is wrong but cannot generate the emotional energy to address it.

**Destroys motivation.** Prolactin suppresses dopamine signaling. Dopamine is the neurotransmitter of wanting, seeking, and motivated behavior. When dopamine is suppressed by prolactin, the drive to pursue goals, create things, build relationships, or even maintain basic self-care erodes. The person becomes passive. This looks like laziness from the outside but feels like paralysis from the inside.

**Promotes weight gain.** Prolactin increases appetite, promotes fat storage (particularly visceral and breast tissue fat), and impairs insulin sensitivity. In men, elevated prolactin contributes to gynecomastia -- the development of breast tissue -- both directly and through its suppression of testosterone.

**Causes gynecomastia.** In men, prolactin directly stimulates breast tissue growth, compounding the effect of elevated estrogen. This is why gynecomastia is so common in conditions where both prolactin and estrogen are elevated -- obesity, hypothyroidism, SSRI use, and chronic stress.

**Impairs immune function.** While prolactin has complex immunomodulatory effects, chronically elevated levels are associated with autoimmune activation. Hyperprolactinemia is more common in patients with lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, and other autoimmune conditions.

## The Dopamine-Prolactin Axis

Prolactin and dopamine exist in a reciprocal relationship. Dopamine is the primary inhibitor of prolactin release. The tuberoinfundibular dopamine pathway runs from the hypothalamus to the pituitary, and its tonic activity keeps prolactin suppressed. When dopamine signaling is strong, prolactin is low, and the person feels motivated, engaged, and capable of experiencing pleasure.

When anything suppresses dopamine -- stress, estrogen, serotonin, hypothyroidism, or dopamine receptor-blocking drugs -- prolactin rises, and the motivational system collapses.

This is the mechanism behind the side effects of antipsychotic medications. Every antipsychotic drug blocks dopamine D2 receptors. This removes the brake on prolactin. Patients on antipsychotics almost universally develop hyperprolactinemia, and with it: weight gain, sexual dysfunction, gynecomastia, emotional blunting, and metabolic syndrome. These are not "side effects" in the usual sense. They are the direct and predictable consequences of removing dopaminergic inhibition of prolactin.

That the psychiatric establishment continues to treat these as acceptable trade-offs reveals a fundamental failure to understand what prolactin does to quality of life.

## The Modern Prolactin Epidemic

Consider the convergence of factors in modern life that all raise prolactin:

- Chronic stress from economic precarity, social comparison, information overload
- Estrogen exposure from plastics, pesticides, oral contraceptives in water
- High serotonin from SSRI prescriptions (now taken by 1 in 8 Americans)
- Darkness -- most people spend their days in dim offices and their evenings in artificially lit rooms
- Hypothyroidism -- increasingly common due to PUFA consumption, fluoride, bromide exposure
- Sleep deprivation -- which raises both cortisol and prolactin

The result is a population with chronically elevated prolactin. They have low libido. They cannot sustain motivation. They gain weight easily and lose it with difficulty. They feel emotionally flat. They scroll social media for hours because they cannot generate the dopaminergic drive to do anything more demanding. They are prescribed SSRIs for their "depression," which raises their prolactin further.

This is not a crisis of character or willpower. It is a hormonal state that can be measured, understood, and corrected.

## What Lowers Prolactin

**Dopamine and dopaminergic support.** The pharmaceutical approach is cabergoline or bromocriptine, both dopamine agonists that directly suppress prolactin. These are effective but carry risks. Natural dopamine support includes adequate protein (for tyrosine, the dopamine precursor), adequate B6 (needed for DOPA decarboxylase), and iron (needed for tyrosine hydroxylase).

**Vitamin E.** Tocopherol has anti-prolactin effects, likely through its anti-estrogenic action and its protection of dopaminergic neurons from oxidative damage. Peat recommended vitamin E regularly, partly for this reason.

**Vitamin B6 (pyridoxal-5-phosphate/P5P).** B6 is a cofactor in dopamine synthesis and has direct prolactin-lowering effects. It has been used clinically for hyperprolactinemia at doses of 200-600mg daily, though Peat's recommendations were more moderate. The active form P5P is preferred over pyridoxine, which requires hepatic conversion.

**Thyroid hormone.** Correcting hypothyroidism normalizes TRH, which normalizes prolactin. This is often the most important single intervention for people with elevated prolactin, because hypothyroidism is so common and so frequently undiagnosed.

**Bright light.** Morning bright light exposure (ideally sunlight, but bright artificial light works) suppresses prolactin directly. This is one reason why morning sun exposure has such reliable mood-boosting effects. It is not just about vitamin D or circadian rhythm -- it is directly suppressing the hormone of passivity and resignation.

**Reducing estrogen.** Every measure that reduces estrogen -- lowering body fat (adipose tissue aromatizes testosterone to estrogen), reducing PUFA (which promotes aromatase activity), avoiding xenoestrogens, supplementing progesterone or vitamin E -- indirectly lowers prolactin.

**Reducing serotonin.** Avoiding unnecessary SSRI use, reducing excessive tryptophan intake (particularly from supplements), and using anti-serotonin agents like cyproheptadine all help keep prolactin in check.

## The Practical Takeaway

If you lack motivation, feel emotionally numb, have low libido, gain weight easily, and struggle to engage with life, the conventional explanation is that you are depressed and need an SSRI. The prolactin framework suggests the opposite: you may already have too much serotonin driving too much prolactin, and what you need is dopaminergic support, thyroid optimization, estrogen reduction, bright light, and vitamin E.

Prolactin is measurable with a standard blood test. A morning fasting level above 15 ng/mL in men or 25 ng/mL in women (non-pregnant) should prompt investigation. But even levels within "normal" range can be supraoptimal, particularly if symptoms are present.

The difference between a person with optimally low prolactin and a person with chronically elevated prolactin is the difference between someone who wakes up wanting to build something and someone who wakes up wanting to stay in bed. This is not willpower. This is biochemistry.
