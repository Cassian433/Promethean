# Supplement Principles: Targeted Metabolic Support

Ray Peat's approach to supplementation was the opposite of the "megadose everything" philosophy that dominates the supplement industry. He viewed supplements as targeted interventions to correct specific metabolic deficiencies or to counteract specific harmful processes. Each supplement he recommended connects directly to his core framework of supporting mitochondrial respiration, suppressing stress hormones, and opposing the anti-metabolic effects of PUFAs, estrogen, serotonin, and iron.

The order below roughly reflects priority — addressing the most foundational issues first.

## Vitamin E: The Anti-PUFA Shield

Vitamin E sits at the top because PUFAs are the most persistent metabolic toxin in the modern body. Stored PUFAs undergo lipid peroxidation, generating reactive aldehydes that damage mitochondrial membranes, DNA, and proteins. Vitamin E (alpha-tocopherol in particular) is a chain-breaking antioxidant that directly intercepts lipid peroxyl radicals, halting the peroxidation cascade.

Beyond its antioxidant role, vitamin E suppresses prolactin. Prolactin is a stress hormone that Peat associated with tumor promotion, immune suppression, and estrogen amplification. Vitamin E also opposes some of estrogen's effects directly. Peat recommended natural mixed tocopherols, typically 200-800 IU daily depending on individual PUFA load and whether active fat loss is occurring. During PUFA detoxification, higher doses provide more protection against the released polyunsaturated fats entering the bloodstream.

## Vitamin D3 + K2: Calcium Metabolism and Immune Regulation

Vitamin D3 functions as a hormone that regulates calcium absorption, immune function, and gene expression. Deficiency is widespread, especially in northern latitudes, and Peat recognized its importance for maintaining the calcium-phosphate balance he considered central to cellular health.

Vitamin K2 (MK-4 form preferred) directs calcium into bones and teeth and away from soft tissues. Without adequate K2, supplemental vitamin D can increase calcium absorption without ensuring it goes where it should — potentially contributing to arterial calcification. The D3/K2 combination ensures calcium metabolism works correctly: absorbed from the gut, deposited in bones, kept out of arteries.

Peat framework targets: vitamin D levels of 40-60 ng/mL. Supplemental doses typically range from 2000-5000 IU D3 daily, with K2 (MK-4) at 1-5 mg daily.

## Magnesium: The Ubiquitous Cofactor

Magnesium participates in over 300 enzymatic reactions including ATP synthesis, DNA repair, protein synthesis, and neurotransmitter regulation. Deficiency is common because modern agriculture depletes soil magnesium and processed food removes it.

In Peat's framework, magnesium is particularly important because it opposes excess calcium inside cells. While dietary calcium is beneficial (suppressing PTH), intracellular calcium excess is a hallmark of stressed and dying cells. Magnesium helps maintain the proper intracellular calcium-to-magnesium ratio. It also supports sleep, reduces muscle tension, and aids bowel regularity.

Forms matter: magnesium glycinate or magnesium bicarbonate (made by dissolving milk of magnesia in carbonated water — a method Peat mentioned) are well-absorbed. Magnesium oxide is poorly absorbed but can be useful as a mild laxative. Typical intake: 200-600 mg elemental magnesium daily, split between doses.

## Aspirin: Anti-Serotonin and Anti-Inflammatory

Peat recommended aspirin not as a painkiller but as a metabolic tool. Aspirin irreversibly inhibits cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, blocking the conversion of PUFAs (especially arachidonic acid) into inflammatory prostaglandins and thromboxanes. Since stored PUFAs are constantly being released and converted into these inflammatory mediators, aspirin provides ongoing protection.

Aspirin also has anti-serotonin effects. It reduces platelet serotonin release and has been shown to lower circulating serotonin. Given Peat's view of serotonin as a stress and inflammation mediator rather than a feel-good molecule, this was a significant benefit.

Additional effects Peat valued: aspirin improves mitochondrial function, has anti-cancer properties (epidemiological data is strong), reduces cortisol, and protects against endotoxin. He typically recommended small doses — 100 to 325 mg, taken with food to protect the stomach lining. Taking aspirin with food that contains some fat and bicarbonate (or dissolved in carbonated water) reduces gastric irritation.

## Pregnenolone: Bypassing the Cortisol Steal

Pregnenolone is the first hormone synthesized from cholesterol and the precursor to all other steroid hormones — progesterone, DHEA, cortisol, testosterone, estradiol, and aldosterone. Under stress, the body preferentially converts pregnenolone down the cortisol pathway (the "pregnenolone steal"), leaving less available for progesterone, DHEA, and downstream hormones.

Supplementing pregnenolone directly provides the raw material without requiring cholesterol conversion (which itself requires thyroid hormone and vitamin A). It supports memory, reduces inflammation, and helps normalize the balance between protective hormones (progesterone, DHEA) and stress hormones (cortisol). Typical doses range from 10-100 mg daily. Peat noted that pregnenolone has an extremely low toxicity profile.

## Progesterone in Vitamin E

Progesterone is the central protective hormone in Peat's framework. It opposes estrogen, supports GABA receptors (producing calming effects), is anti-inflammatory, protects myelin sheaths, and supports mitochondrial function. Peat recommended progesterone dissolved in vitamin E — this combination provides both the hormone and its protective carrier simultaneously.

For men, progesterone is relevant because it opposes estrogen and supports brain function, though doses are typically lower than for women. For women, progesterone deficiency relative to estrogen (estrogen dominance) is one of the most common hormonal imbalances and is associated with PMS, fibroids, endometriosis, anxiety, and insomnia.

Progesterone dissolved in vitamin E is applied topically or taken orally. Topical application on thin skin (inner wrists, behind ears) allows direct absorption. Oral administration results in significant first-pass liver metabolism, producing neurosteroid metabolites that have potent GABA-activating (calming, sleep-promoting) effects. Peat considered both routes useful depending on the goal.

## Thiamine: TTFD and Allithiamine

Thiamine (vitamin B1) is essential for pyruvate dehydrogenase, the enzyme complex that converts pyruvate into acetyl-CoA for entry into the Krebs cycle. Without adequate thiamine, pyruvate accumulates and is converted to lactate — a metabolic dead end that Peat associated with cancer metabolism (the Warburg effect), fatigue, and acidosis.

TTFD (thiamine tetrahydrofurfuryl disulfide) and allithiamine are fat-soluble forms of thiamine that cross the blood-brain barrier more effectively than water-soluble thiamine HCl. This matters because the brain's metabolic demands for thiamine are exceptionally high, and deficiency manifests as cognitive impairment, anxiety, and autonomic dysfunction long before clinical beriberi symptoms appear.

Peat recognized that subclinical thiamine deficiency is far more common than diagnosed, especially in people consuming high-carbohydrate diets (which increase thiamine demand) or alcohol. Doses of TTFD typically range from 50-100 mg daily.

## B Vitamins: The Full Spectrum

Beyond thiamine, the full B-vitamin complex supports energy metabolism at every level. Riboflavin (B2) is a component of FAD, essential for Complex II of the electron transport chain. Niacin (B3) is a component of NAD+, required for virtually every oxidative reaction. Pyridoxine (B6) is involved in amino acid metabolism and neurotransmitter synthesis. B12 and folate support methylation, DNA synthesis, and red blood cell production.

Peat did not advocate megadosing individual B vitamins in isolation (with the exception of thiamine) but supported adequate intake through diet (liver, eggs, milk) supplemented with a balanced B-complex when needed.

## Zinc, Copper, and Selenium: Trace Mineral Balance

These three trace minerals form an interconnected system. Zinc supports thyroid function, testosterone production, immune function, and over 300 enzymatic processes. Copper is required for ceruloplasmin (which makes iron bioavailable and prevents free iron accumulation), cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV of the electron transport chain), and dopamine synthesis. Selenium is essential for thyroid hormone conversion (T4 to T3 via deiodinase enzymes) and for glutathione peroxidase.

The balance between zinc and copper is critical — excess zinc depletes copper and vice versa. Peat recommended getting copper from food sources (liver, shellfish, chocolate) rather than supplements when possible, as supplemental copper can be irritating.

## Thyroid Supplementation

When dietary and lifestyle interventions are insufficient to restore metabolic rate, Peat supported direct thyroid supplementation. He preferred T3 (cytomel/liothyronine) or natural desiccated thyroid (NDT, such as Armour or similar) over T4-only preparations (levothyroxine/Synthroid). His reasoning: many people have impaired T4-to-T3 conversion due to selenium deficiency, high cortisol, iron dysregulation, or PUFA-mediated suppression of deiodinase enzymes. Giving T4 alone does not address the conversion problem.

T3 is the active thyroid hormone that directly increases cellular respiration. Peat recommended starting with very small doses (5-10 mcg) and titrating upward based on symptoms, temperature, and pulse. The goal is to support metabolism to the point where the body can eventually maintain its own thyroid production — not to create dependence on exogenous hormone.

## Cyproheptadine: The Anti-Serotonin Tool

Cyproheptadine is a prescription antihistamine that also potently blocks serotonin receptors. Peat recommended it for situations where serotonin excess was driving symptoms — insomnia, anxiety, digestive disturbance (serotonin is heavily concentrated in the gut), migraine, and carcinoid-like symptoms.

It also stimulates appetite and weight gain, which Peat considered beneficial in underweight or hypometabolic individuals but a drawback for others. Cyproheptadine represents the more interventionist end of Peat's supplement framework — used when dietary and lifestyle measures alone are insufficient to manage serotonin-driven pathology.

## The Connecting Logic

Every supplement in this framework addresses a specific bottleneck in mitochondrial energy production or a specific harmful process that suppresses it. Vitamin E protects against PUFA peroxidation. Aspirin blocks inflammatory prostaglandin synthesis from PUFAs. Thyroid hormone directly increases the rate of oxidative metabolism. Progesterone opposes the anti-metabolic effects of estrogen. Pregnenolone prevents stress-hormone steal. Magnesium, B vitamins, and trace minerals provide the cofactors that mitochondrial enzymes require.

This is not a supplement stack to take blindly. It is a set of tools, each addressing a specific mechanism, to be used based on individual assessment of which mechanisms are most impaired. Blood work, symptom tracking, and temperature/pulse monitoring guide which supplements are most needed and at what doses.
