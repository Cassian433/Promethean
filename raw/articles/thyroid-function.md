# Thyroid Function: The Master Metabolic Regulator

## The Thermostat of the Body

If oxidative metabolism is the engine, thyroid hormone is the thermostat. It sets the rate at which every cell in the body produces energy. When thyroid function is adequate, body temperature is maintained, hormones are produced, the brain works, the gut moves, wounds heal, and the immune system responds appropriately. When thyroid function is low, everything slows down — and the downstream effects are so varied that they get diagnosed as dozens of separate conditions.

Ray Peat considered thyroid function so central to health that nearly every topic he wrote about eventually circled back to it. Understanding thyroid is understanding the control system that governs metabolic rate.

## T4, T3, and the Conversion That Matters

The thyroid gland primarily produces **thyroxine (T4)** — a prohormone with four iodine atoms. T4 is relatively inactive. It circulates in the blood as a reservoir, waiting to be converted into the active form.

**Triiodothyronine (T3)** is the active thyroid hormone. It is produced by removing one iodine atom from T4 via **deiodinase enzymes** (primarily Type 1 and Type 2). This conversion happens mainly in the liver, kidneys, and peripheral tissues.

T3 is what enters the cell nucleus, binds to thyroid hormone receptors, and directly upregulates gene expression for mitochondrial proteins, metabolic enzymes, and energy-producing machinery. Without adequate T3, the cell's metabolic machinery runs at reduced capacity.

The distinction matters clinically because many people have adequate T4 levels but impaired conversion to T3. Their lab work looks normal by conventional standards, but their cells are starving for the active hormone.

## What T3 Upregulates

The effects of adequate T3 are system-wide:

**Metabolic rate and body temperature.** T3 directly increases mitochondrial respiration. More ATP is produced, more heat is generated. Basal body temperature of 98.6F / 37C upon waking and reaching 98.8-99F by afternoon is a sign of good thyroid function.

**5-alpha reductase activity.** T3 upregulates the enzyme that converts testosterone to DHT (dihydrotestosterone), the most potent androgen. This is why hypothyroid men often have low libido, poor assertiveness, and weak sexual function — not because testosterone is low, but because T3-dependent conversion to DHT is impaired.

**Progesterone production.** Thyroid hormone is required for the conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone in the mitochondria — the first step in steroid hormone synthesis. Low thyroid means low pregnenolone, low progesterone, and a shift toward estrogen dominance.

**Cholesterol metabolism.** T3 upregulates LDL receptors on the liver, clearing cholesterol from the blood. Hypothyroidism is one of the most common causes of elevated cholesterol — and one of the most overlooked. Broda Barnes documented this extensively.

**Gut motility.** T3 maintains the muscular contractions of the intestine. Low thyroid function leads to constipation, bacterial overgrowth, and increased endotoxin absorption.

**Immune function.** Thyroid hormone is required for proper immune cell maturation and function. Hypothyroid individuals are more susceptible to infections and slower to recover.

**Brain function.** The brain is exquisitely sensitive to thyroid status. Low T3 manifests as brain fog, poor memory, slow thinking, depression, and anxiety. Many cases of treatment-resistant depression are undiagnosed hypothyroidism.

## Broda Barnes and Basal Temperature

Dr. Broda Barnes spent decades studying thyroid function and published extensively on the inadequacy of blood tests for diagnosing hypothyroidism. His method was simple: measure basal body temperature (axillary, upon waking, before getting out of bed) and basal pulse rate.

Barnes found that:

- **Basal temperature below 97.8F** strongly suggests hypothyroidism, regardless of what blood tests show.
- **Basal pulse below 70** (in a person who is not aerobically trained) also suggests low metabolic rate.
- **Cold hands and feet** are among the most reliable clinical signs.
- **Elevated cholesterol** in the context of low temperature is almost certainly thyroid-related.

Barnes treated thousands of patients with desiccated thyroid (which contains both T4 and T3) and documented dramatic improvements in cardiovascular disease, infections, and overall mortality — outcomes that later TSH-guided treatment with synthetic T4 alone failed to replicate.

Peat agreed with Barnes' clinical approach and considered temperature and pulse rate far more reliable indicators of thyroid status than blood tests.

## Why TSH Alone Is Insufficient

Conventional medicine relies heavily on **TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone)** to assess thyroid function. The logic: if the thyroid is underperforming, the pituitary releases more TSH to stimulate it. High TSH = hypothyroid. Normal TSH = normal thyroid.

This model is simplistic and misses several critical problems:

**The reference range is too wide.** "Normal" TSH ranges from roughly 0.5 to 4.5 mIU/L in most labs. A TSH of 4.0 is treated as normal despite being associated with significantly increased cardiovascular risk, elevated cholesterol, and symptoms of hypothyroidism. Optimal TSH is closer to 1.0-2.0.

**TSH does not reflect tissue-level T3.** A person can have normal TSH and T4 but poor conversion to T3 in peripheral tissues. The pituitary has its own deiodinase and may be adequately supplied with T3 even when the rest of the body is not.

**Stress hormones suppress TSH.** Cortisol and dopamine both suppress TSH secretion. A stressed, cortisol-dominant person can have low TSH despite genuine hypothyroidism at the tissue level. Their labs look "normal" or even hyperthyroid while they are functionally hypothyroid.

**Reverse T3 complicates the picture.** Under stress, illness, or caloric restriction, the body preferentially converts T4 to **reverse T3 (rT3)** instead of T3. Reverse T3 is biologically inactive — it occupies thyroid receptors without activating them, effectively acting as a brake on metabolism. High rT3 with normal T4 and TSH is a common pattern in chronic illness and goes completely undetected by standard testing.

## What Suppresses Thyroid Function

Multiple factors converge to suppress the thyroid axis in modern life:

**Polyunsaturated fats.** PUFAs inhibit T4-to-T3 conversion, block thyroid hormone transport on binding proteins, and directly interfere with thyroid receptor function. This is one of the primary mechanisms by which seed oils damage health.

**Excess estrogen.** Estrogen increases thyroid-binding globulin (TBG), which binds thyroid hormone in the blood and makes it unavailable to tissues. A woman on birth control pills or a man with high aromatase activity can have normal total T4 but low free T4 and T3 due to elevated TBG.

**Cortisol and chronic stress.** Cortisol promotes the conversion of T4 to reverse T3 instead of T3. It also directly suppresses TSH. The stressed person is in a metabolic trap: stress lowers thyroid function, low thyroid increases stress vulnerability, and the cycle deepens.

**Caloric restriction and low-carbohydrate diets.** The liver requires glucose and insulin to efficiently convert T4 to T3. Low-carb and fasting diets reliably reduce T3 levels. The metabolic slowdown people experience on prolonged caloric restriction is substantially thyroid-mediated.

**Fluoride and other halogens.** Fluoride competes with iodine at the thyroid gland and was historically used as an anti-thyroid drug. Bromide (found in some commercial bread and flame retardants) and chlorine have similar effects.

**Endotoxin.** Bacterial lipopolysaccharide from the gut suppresses thyroid function through inflammatory cytokines. Poor gut health directly impairs thyroid function.

## Symptoms That Get Misdiagnosed

Because thyroid hormone affects every system, low thyroid presents in ways that look like dozens of other conditions:

- **Depression and anxiety** — prescribed SSRIs instead of thyroid support
- **High cholesterol** — prescribed statins instead of investigating thyroid
- **Fatigue** — attributed to depression, poor sleep hygiene, or "stress"
- **Weight gain** — attributed to overeating and lack of exercise
- **Constipation** — treated with fiber supplements
- **Hair loss** — attributed to genetics or "telogen effluvium"
- **Dry skin** — treated with moisturizers
- **Infertility** — extensive workup without checking free T3 or basal temperature
- **Cold intolerance** — considered a personality quirk
- **Menstrual irregularities** — treated with birth control pills (which further suppress thyroid via estrogen)
- **Slow healing** — not investigated
- **Carpal tunnel syndrome** — Broda Barnes documented its resolution with thyroid treatment

The pattern is consistent: each symptom is treated in isolation with a targeted pharmaceutical, while the underlying metabolic insufficiency goes unaddressed.

## Practical Assessment and Support

**Self-assessment:** Take oral temperature upon waking (before getting out of bed) and again in mid-afternoon. Waking temperature should be 97.8F or above; afternoon temperature should reach 98.6F or higher. Take resting pulse at the same times — 75-85 bpm in a relaxed, warm state suggests adequate metabolic rate.

**Blood work worth requesting:** Free T3 (not just total T3), free T4, TSH, and reverse T3. The ratio of free T3 to reverse T3 is more informative than any single value.

**Dietary support:** Adequate carbohydrate intake (especially from fruit and orange juice) supports T4-to-T3 conversion. Selenium (from Brazil nuts or supplements) is a cofactor for deiodinase enzymes. Iodine from seafood and dairy supports thyroid hormone production. Vitamin A (retinol, from liver and dairy) is required for thyroid hormone receptor function.

**Remove suppressors:** Reduce PUFA intake, manage stress, support gut health, ensure adequate caloric intake. Do not chronically restrict calories or carbohydrates if thyroid function is compromised.

The thyroid axis is not one system among many — it is the system that sets the pace for all others. Getting it right does not solve every problem, but getting it wrong guarantees that nothing else works optimally.
